Event Overview

US–MENA PSD 2026 | 20th Anniversary Edition

It is a closed, senior-level dialogue built around regulatory trust, financial integrity and the future of US–MENA banking connectivity.

US–MENA PSD 2026 marks the landmark 20th anniversary edition of one of the most influential banking and regulatory dialogue platforms connecting the United States and the MENA region.

Taking place in New York, the conference convenes an exclusive senior audience of banking leaders, central bank authorities, U.S. regulatory stakeholders, correspondent banking decision-makers, financial crime specialists and compliance technology providers.

The platform has featured participation from major institutions including the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, U.S. Department of the Treasury, FinCEN, OFAC, IMF, FATF, MENAFATF, INTERPOL, Union of Arab Banks, BNY, Standard Chartered, Wells Fargo, K2 Integrity, Clifford Chance and Squire Patton Boggs.

Focused on AML/CFT, sanctions, correspondent banking access, cross-border payments, digital finance, AI-enabled compliance and high-risk jurisdictions, US–MENA PSD is not a conventional banking conference.

Why This Dialogue Matters

A senior-level regulatory dialogue on financial integrity and cross-border banking access.

US-MENA Private Sector Dialogue 2026 convenes senior public-sector, central banking, financial intelligence, correspondent banking, and compliance leadership to address the regulatory expectations shaping access to the global financial system.

01

Regulatory Trust

U.S. expectations are moving toward measurable, risk-based, and defensible compliance programs with clear board and senior management accountability.

02

Correspondent Banking Access

Regional Risk Complexity

Agenda and Strategic Discussion Themes

Strengthening US–MENA Financial Integrity, Correspondent Banking Access & Cross-Border Compliance

US–MENA PSD 2026 brings together senior banking leaders, regulators, compliance decision-makers, correspondent banking executives and financial crime specialists to address the critical regulatory and market issues shaping MENA banks’ access to the global financial system.

01

The New U.S. AML/CFT Compliance Landscape

Treasury, FinCEN & Governance Expectations

Why it matters: Banks must prove not only that controls exist, but that they are effective, risk-based and defensible.

Key discussion points
  • –Shift from technical compliance to outcome-based AML/CFT programs
  • –FinCEN expectations for risk-based, measurable and effective controls
  • –Board-level oversight and senior management accountability
  • –Enterprise-wide compliance governance and institutional resilience
  • –Integrating U.S. AML/CFT priorities into bank risk assessments
  • –Beneficial ownership, complex structures and hidden control risks
  • –Legal defensibility of AML/CFT and sanctions compliance programs
02

Safeguarding Correspondent Banking Access

Transparency, KYCC & Cross-Border Visibility

Why it matters: Correspondent banking access remains a strategic lifeline for MENA banks, trade flows and financial connectivity.

Key discussion points
  • –Preserving correspondent banking relationships under higher scrutiny
  • –U.S. correspondent bank expectations on transparency and governance
  • –KYCC and payment-chain visibility
  • –Understanding underlying counterparties and transaction ecosystems
  • –Managing de-risking pressures while preserving financial access
  • –Cash economies, informal channels, MSBs and shadow banking risks
03

Syria, Iraq & High-Risk Jurisdictions

Practical Risk Management Without Political Positioning

Why it matters: Banks need practical frameworks to support permitted activity while managing sanctions, trade and reputational exposure.

Key discussion points
  • –Managing exposure to complex jurisdictions through risk-based controls
  • –Humanitarian channels and permitted financial flows
  • –Reconstruction-related transactions and compliance safeguards
  • –Syria’s evolving trade, compliance and financial landscape
  • –Export controls, dual-use goods and restricted party screening
  • –Readiness gaps in compliance programs across high-risk markets
04

Trade, Oil, Shipping & Financial Crime Risk

Commodity Flows, Maritime Exposure & Sanctions Circumvention

Why it matters: Trade, oil and shipping channels are increasingly used to disguise financial crime, sanctions evasion and illicit flows.

Key discussion points
  • –Trade-based money laundering and false documentation
  • –Invoice manipulation, trade mispricing and false origin declarations
  • –Maritime due diligence and vessel ownership transparency
  • –Shipping routes, vessel monitoring and sanctions exposure
  • –Regional maritime disruptions and trade finance implications
  • –Oil payments, commodity transactions and intermediary risks
  • –Supply-chain diversion and dual-use goods exposure
05

Sanctions, Iran-Linked Risks & Regional Escalation

Managing Hidden Exposure in a Volatile Environment

Why it matters: Sanctions risk is no longer limited to named parties; exposure can emerge through indirect links, trade routes and hidden networks.

Key discussion points
  • –Sanctions compliance in a rapidly changing regional environment
  • –Emerging sanctions evasion typologies and risk indicators
  • –Intermediary networks, front companies and alternative payment channels
  • –Secondary sanctions and indirect exposure
  • –Risks linked to customers, vessels, insurers, counterparties and trade routes
  • –OFAC expectations for shipping and safe-passage payments
  • –Digital assets and sanctions evasion risks
  • –Corruption, illicit networks and financial integrity exposure
06

AI, Digital Finance & Compliance Technology

Building Future-Ready Compliance Controls

Why it matters: Technology can strengthen compliance, but only when supported by governance, explainability and regulatory confidence.

Key discussion points
  • –Digital assets, stablecoins and evolving AML/CFT expectations
  • –VASPs and cross-border digital asset transfers
  • –Cyber-enabled financial crime and sanctions evasion
  • –Travel Rule compliance and payment transparency
  • –AI-backed controls for AML/CFT, sanctions and financial crime compliance
  • –Governance, suitability and oversight of AI-enabled compliance tools
  • –Banks, fintechs, VASPs and MSBs in the evolving financial ecosystem
